Data Science Project

A project exploring the effect on pay on investment banks' performance

Introduction

One of the most fundemental questions any employer must ask is how much to pay an employee. Microeconomic theory would suggest that employees must be paid just above their no-shirking level: the point where the wage is high enough that losing one's job would be more costly than exerting effort. In practice, this is an impossible number to reach, and in any case the model is too simplistic.

Careers in the City are no exception to this, and have long been renowned for paying large amounts. With the on-going war on talent, as well as burnout during the panemic, banks have further raised their salaries (in the region of 30%) this year for all their junior roles. But does this provide value for money? This project aims to establish a link between the pay for junior roles in investment banks with their performance, measured by the fees the bank brings in each year.

Unfortunately, banks do not actively publish their annual fees and how much they pay their employees. Furthermore, data providors, such as Refinitiv, which publish APIs containing this information, charge thousands each yaer for access. This project therefore scrapes free resources avalaible to anyone in the public to try establish a link. Please note: this project does not aim to establish causality - the performance of banks is such a complicated field - but merely tries to establish a link.

Section 1: What are the major global investment banks
This first chart shows the performance of the top 10 investment banks globally, based on total fee value. This graph outlines the major players in the space - it is these banks whose pay will be compared to their performance. The data is from the FT, scraped from their website. Sadly the FT only publishes the top 10 banks, but it is a free resource which can be updated very quickly. The scraper can be found here.
Section 2: Pay landscape for Investment Bankers in London
Arkesden is a head hunter, based in London, which recruits for Private Equity, Real Estate, Alternatives, Energy and (crucially), Investment Banking. Each year they produce a free-to-download PDF report summarising pay in each sector. A scraper has been built to scrape this PDF, meaning that each year when the report is published, the charts can be updated.

The latest PDF (2020-2021) does not reflect the recent increase in base salary, but the scraper has been designed to be fully automated, meaning when the latest report comes out updating the data will be a quick process. The link for the scraper is here.

The two charts above summarise base pay (excluding bonuses, which often make up a sizable proportion of a bankers' salary). From immediate inspection, it is clear that Bank of Merrill Lynch (BAML) pays more than other banks, especially at the Analyst rank (the lowest level), and we will test if this translates to greater performance.
The chart above shows the total compensation, including both base pay and bonus pay. Bonus is a function of the performance of the individual (what we are testing) and performance of the bank, an example of reverse causality, hence why this project is not aiming to infer causality. The data from the chart was again scraped from Arkesden and can be replicated here.
